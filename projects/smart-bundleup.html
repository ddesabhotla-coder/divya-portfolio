Smart BundleUp

Context

Schneider Electric's smart and sustainable home products were built for the future—but adoption depended on builders who were deeply rooted in traditional, experience-driven practices.

While business partners saw smart technology as a strategic differentiator, many builders viewed it as unnecessary change. Not because it slowed them down or didn't work, but because it didn't yet feel like a natural part of how homes were built. The result was a disconnect:

  • Business teams pushed for innovation and market entry
  • Builders prioritized familiarity, predictability, and proven workflows

Without addressing this gap, even strong products risked being underutilized or ignored.

Problem Statement

The primary challenge was bridging the gap between Schneider Electric's market expansion goals and the practical, experience-driven priorities of new home builders, ensuring adoption felt beneficial rather than burdensome.

Business Partner

"Smart and sustainable homes are where the market is heading. If we don't convince the builders to adopt our technology now, we risk losing an entire generation of homeowners to our competitors."

"I've been building homes the same way for years—and my clients are happy. I'm not against new tech, I just don't see why I need to change what already works."

New Home Builder

How might we

How might Schneider Electric enter the smart and sustainable home market by designing an experience that fits naturally into new home builders' existing workflows—creating value for both the business and its partners without introducing disruption?

Key Features: Solution

The project positioned smart energy solutions as an extension of what builders already do, not a departure from it. Our solution created a smart bundle of products and a business plan that mutually benefitted all parties involved.

What's in it for each party?

Samsung SmartThings

More and guaranteed sales due to tie up with the production builders.

Subsidized pricing—hello profits. Additionally, tax incentives as homeowners start giving back to the solar grid.

Beachhead Market

Beachhead market was determined by considering the target user group, inclination towards sustainable power consumption, and mandate towards installing solar panels in newly built homes.

Through our research we narrowed down on the age group 25 to 44 due to higher inclination towards smart homes. We identified Southern California for the beachhead market due to the high population of our target age group and Title 24 Compliance, which mandates solar panels.

User Interviews

Ideation & Final Recommendations → Define final go-to-market strategy with both business and user impact in view.

Crazy 8 Ideation

Based on insights from discovery, the team conducted a Crazy 8s ideation session, where each member rapidly generated solution concepts. Ideas were then reviewed and voted on to converge on a clear design direction.

Final Decision

The team recommended partnering with production builders as the primary go-to-market strategy due to its scalability and alignment with builder incentives.

To further strengthen adoption and reduce friction, the final solution combined this approach with a strategic collaboration with Samsung, leveraging:

  • Samsung's established presence in the smart home ecosystem
  • Production builders' ability to scale adoption at build time
  • A subscription-based model that created long-term value for both Schneider Electric and builders

Project Reflections

UX Can Be a Go-to-Market Lever

This project reinforced that UX is not limited to interface design. Some of the most impactful decisions were about who we designed for and when adoption happens, not how screens look. By focusing on builders and installers—before homeowners ever interact with the product—we positioned UX as a driver of market entry, not just usability.

People adopt products when incentives align, not when features impress.

Smart and sustainable technology alone wasn't enough to drive adoption. Builders weren't resistant to innovation—they were comfortable with proven practices. Adoption depended on aligning incentives so the solution felt beneficial without requiring behavioral change.

Ecosystem design is about trade-offs, not perfect experiences.

This work challenged the idea of a single primary user. Builders, business stakeholders, and homeowners all influenced success at different stages. Designing across this ecosystem required balancing competing needs without optimizing exclusively for any one group.

The fastest way to drive change is to hide it inside familiarity.

One of the biggest insights was that innovation is easier to adopt when it feels familiar. Leveraging existing smart-home platforms and builder workflows reduced friction far more effectively than introducing something entirely new.
